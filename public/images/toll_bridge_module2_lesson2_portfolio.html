Building your teaching portfolio.

Your CV is the first thing an employer sees before they ever hear your voice. Let's make sure it says everything it needs to say.

10 min read

I want to show you something honest before we get into what a great teaching CV looks like.

Below is the actual CV I used when I was teaching ESL online. It got me jobs. It worked. But looking at it now, I can see clearly what was missing — and what those gaps probably cost me in terms of opportunities I never even knew I lost.

Use this as your reference point. Not as something to copy, but as something to learn from.

This CV is not bad. But it is not built for a Chinese ESL employer. It was built for a general teaching job. There is a difference — and that difference matters more than most people realize.

An employer reviewing twenty CVs in ten minutes needs to know within five seconds whether you are worth their time. If your CV does not speak their language, you get skipped — and you never even know it happened.

What a strong ESL teaching portfolio actually needs

Here is the complete checklist. Go through every item. Build your CV around this structure. And do not move on to applying until every box is ticked.

1
A professional profile summary
This is the first thing the employer reads. Two to three sentences that tell them immediately who you are, what you teach, who you teach it to, and what makes you different. Not a list of adjectives. A real human statement.
"Experienced ESL teacher with 6+ years teaching English to K12 learners online and in person. TEFL certified. Specializing in building confidence and fluency in young Chinese learners aged 4–12. Conversant with ClassIn, Zoom, and Google Classroom."
2
A professional photo
Clear, well-lit, smiling, professional background. Chinese employers and parents place high value on warmth and approachability. Your photo is doing a job before you say a single word. Do not use a casual selfie. Do not use a group photo cropped. Treat it like a passport photo — but with energy.
3
Your TEFL certification — prominently placed
Do not bury this. It should appear near the top, clearly labelled with the issuing body and date. If you completed the 120-hour TeacherRecord TEFL, state that explicitly. Employers need to verify it — make it easy for them.
TEFL/TESOL Certified — 120 Hours (TeacherRecord, February 2024) · Internationally Recognised
4
Teaching numbers that prove experience
Vague experience descriptions do not impress anyone. Numbers do. How many lessons per month? How many students? How many years? If you recorded 50 lessons a month, say so and do the math — that is 600 lessons a year.
Delivered 600+ recorded online lessons annually. Supported 30+ learners in exam preparation for British and Cambridge school entry.
5
Specific age ranges taught
"K12 learners" means nothing to a Chinese employer. They want to know: can you handle a 4-year-old? A teenager preparing for exams? A working adult? Break it down. Age specificity builds trust.
Ages taught: 4–6 (early learners), 7–12 (primary), 13–17 (secondary exam prep), adult professionals
6
Your intro/demo video link
This is non-negotiable for Chinese ESL employers. Your video is your voice, your energy, your face — everything the paper cannot show. Add a clearly labelled link directly on your CV. If you do not have one yet, this is your most urgent task after finishing this lesson.
Introduction Video: [your YouTube or Google Drive link here]
7
Platform proficiency — specific tools listed
Do not just write "Zoom." List every platform you know and indicate your comfort level. Chinese employers use ClassIn, VoovMeetings, Zoom, and Google Classroom. The more platforms you list, the more deployable you are.
ClassIn (advanced) · VoovMeetings · Zoom · Google Classroom · Microsoft Teams
8
Your teaching approach — in your own words
What kind of teacher are you? High energy? Patient? Structured? Story-based? Add one or two sentences that describe how you actually teach — not just what subjects. This is what makes you memorable versus forgettable.
"My teaching style is warm, structured, and interactive. I use games, songs, and visual aids to keep young learners engaged, and I prioritise building confidence before correcting mistakes."
9
Correct contact details for where you actually are
If you put a UK address but you are teaching from Nigeria, you will confuse employers — or worse, disqualify yourself from opportunities that are open to Nigerian applicants specifically. Use your current location. Be accurate.
10
A curriculum vitae — not a job application form
Your CV should be clean, one to two pages maximum, professionally formatted, easy to scan. No walls of text. No dense paragraphs. Use clear sections, consistent fonts, and enough white space that someone reading it quickly can find what they need. Design matters. First impressions matter.

Beyond the CV — your full teaching portfolio

A portfolio is not just a CV. When you are ready to go beyond the basics, here is what a complete teaching portfolio includes:

1. Your CV — built using everything in the checklist above.

2. Your intro video — two minutes, professional, warm, well-lit. This is your most important asset.

3. Sample lesson plan — one structured lesson plan that shows an employer you know how to prepare, sequence, and deliver a class.

4. Student testimonials or results — if you have taught before, even informally, ask for a written or video testimonial. Evidence of results is gold.

5. Your certificates — TEFL, any other teaching certifications, your university degree. Have digital copies ready to send.

6. A progress tracker sample — showing an employer that you track student progress proves you are serious about outcomes, not just showing up.

Your assignment: Write your professional profile summary first. Two to three sentences. Who you are, what you teach, who you teach it to. That one paragraph will change how employers see everything else on your CV.
