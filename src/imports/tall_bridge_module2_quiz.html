Question 1 of 10Classroom Management
A student goes very quiet mid-class and stops responding. What is the best first response?
Why this is correct

Using the student's name makes them feel seen, and a direct question forces gentle re-engagement. Switching activity resets the energy without pressure or embarrassment.

Question 2 of 10Classroom Management
How often should you change activities during an online class to maintain a child learner's attention?
Why this is correct

Children's online attention spans drop significantly after 8 to 10 minutes. Changing activities — even slightly — resets focus and signals that something new is coming.

Question 3 of 10Nonverbal Communication
During a class, your student starts rubbing their head and repeatedly looking away from the screen. What does this most likely communicate?
Why this is correct

Rubbing the head and looking away are classic nonverbal signals of confusion, discomfort, or mental fatigue. A strong teacher reads these cues and pivots — slowing down, simplifying, or switching activities — before the student disengages completely.

Question 4 of 10Nonverbal Communication
Which of the following is the most effective way to show warmth and build trust with a student through a screen?
Why this is correct

Looking into the camera creates the sensation of eye contact for the student. Combined with a genuine smile, this communicates warmth, safety, and presence — all of which are essential for a child to feel comfortable learning.

Question 5 of 10Verbal Communication
Your student is a beginner with very limited English. How should you adjust your spoken English when teaching them?
Why this is correct

You should never speak in broken or slurred English — this teaches incorrect patterns. Instead, speak naturally but with deliberate clarity: slow your pace, enunciate carefully, use simple words, and repeat key phrases. You are modelling the language they are learning to speak.

Question 6 of 10Verbal Communication
Which question is more likely to generate a full sentence response from a student?
Why this is correct

"Do you understand?" and "Did you get it?" almost always produce a yes — even when the student does not understand. Asking them to USE the word in a sentence requires actual application, which reveals true comprehension and produces a full verbal response.

Question 7 of 10Vocal Delivery
A teacher speaks every sentence at the same speed, same volume, and same pitch throughout the class. What is the most likely effect on the student?
Why this is correct

Monotonous delivery gives the brain nothing to hold onto. Without variation in pitch, pace, or stress, the mind disengages and wanders. Vocal variety — rising for excitement, slowing for emphasis, pausing for impact — is what keeps a listener present and attentive.

Question 8 of 10Vocal Delivery
Which technique best describes stressing certain words in a sentence to direct a student's attention?
Why this is correct

Stress patterns refer to deliberately emphasising certain words to signal importance. For example: "Open your book to page FIVE" tells the student exactly where to focus. Stress patterns are a core tool of effective vocal delivery in teaching.

Question 9 of 10Lesson Planning
What is the primary purpose of a lesson plan before teaching an online class?
Why this is correct

A lesson plan provides structure and intention — but it is not a rigid script. It guides the flow, ensures progression, and allows the teacher to be present rather than improvising. The plan serves the student, not the teacher's ego.

Question 10 of 10Lesson Planning
When planning a lesson for a student you have taught before, what is the most important starting point?
Why this is correct

Great lesson planning is student-centred. Reviewing what the student found difficult in the previous session allows you to address gaps, build confidence, and ensure real progression — rather than simply moving through material.
