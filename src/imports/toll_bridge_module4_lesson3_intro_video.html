Your introductory video.

They have never seen you. They have never heard you. This two-minute video is your entire first impression — make it count.

4 min read

Before an employer ever puts you in front of their student, they watch your introductory video. In most cases, that video is the only thing standing between you and the job. There is no cover letter. There is no lengthy interview. Just you, a camera, and however much of yourself you are willing to show in two minutes.

This video was used to win over more than fifty parents and employers across different countries. Watch it. Then read the rest of this lesson.

Your introductory video is not a CV read aloud. It is a performance. It is your energy, your warmth, your confidence, and your passion — compressed into two minutes.

What employers and parents are actually watching for

When an employer presses play on your video, they are not listening for a list of qualifications. They are asking themselves one question: would I trust this person with my child?

That question is answered within the first fifteen seconds. Your appearance, your energy, how you smile, how you speak — all of that lands before you have even finished your opening sentence.

Your energy
Are you switched on and genuinely excited? A flat, rehearsed tone will lose an employer immediately. Bring warmth. Bring life. Speak like you actually love what you do.
Your appearance
Clean, professional, approachable. You do not need to be in formal attire — but you do need to look like someone a parent would invite into their child's learning space.
Your spoken English
Clear, confident, natural. Not over-performed. Not robotic. Speak the way you would in a real class — with pacing, warmth, and intention behind every sentence.
Your commitment to students
Say something specific about how you teach and what you are committed to delivering. Not vague words like "I am passionate" — but real statements about what a student will experience in your class.
Your teaching style
Are you playful and interactive? Do you use games and visuals? Do you focus on conversation or structure? Tell them. Parents want to know their child will enjoy the lessons — not just survive them.

What to say — and what not to say

Say: What makes you good at teaching. Your experience. Your methods. Your energy. Your specific commitment to the learner's success. Why you love it.

Do not say: That you are strict. That you expect perfection. That your students must work hard. Chinese parents want a teacher who delivers results — but they also want a teacher their child is excited to learn from. Balance matters. Lead with warmth, follow with capability.

The practical checklist

Keep it under two minutes — three minutes maximum
Employers watch dozens of videos. If yours is four or five minutes long, they will not finish it. Say everything you need to say concisely and stop.
Use your noise-cancelling headset
Background noise ruins an otherwise great video. Clear, crisp audio signals professionalism immediately.
Film in a well-lit, clean environment
Natural light from in front of you is ideal. Your background should look like a place a child would feel comfortable learning in. Coworking spaces work perfectly if your home setup is not ready.
Smile — genuinely and often
Chinese parents respond strongly to warmth. A teacher who smiles is a teacher their child will want to come back to. Your smile is doing more work than your words.
Look directly into the camera
Not at your screen — at the camera. That is eye contact on video. It makes employers feel like you are speaking directly to them.
Record multiple takes
Your first take is almost never your best. Record three to five versions. Watch them back. Pick the one where you sound most natural — not most rehearsed.

Your assignment: Record your first practice intro video today. Do not wait for perfect lighting or perfect words. Just turn the camera on and talk. Watch it back. What did you do well? What felt flat? That honest self-assessment is how you arrive at a video that wins employers over.
